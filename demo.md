# Aftercall — Submission / Narrative Video (≤90s)

A tight, captioned, hard-cut screen capture that **reads on mute** — captions
carry every Dial primitive so judges scoring without sound still see the depth.
Record during the freeze hour, not at the buzzer.

> **Length note:** `hackathon.md` records the *submission* requirement as **≤60s**.
> This file is cut to **≤90s** (extra room for the emotional cold open) with a
> **60s fallback marked inline** — at freeze hour, decide which one ships as the
> submission. The 90s cut doubles as the extended/landing-page video.

This video opens with a **narrative cold open**: a person, in distress after the
crisis → the help line that leaves her on hold → **she calls/texts Aftercall, and
someone finally answers.**

## The core insight the hook is built on

When you need someone most, the old line is **busy** — hold music, "all our
representatives are unavailable," no one on the other end. Aftercall is the line
that **always picks up**: text it or call it, any hour, and a calm voice answers
in seconds, supports you, and pulls in a human the moment you're in real distress.

The cold open dramatizes the **help that never comes**, then delivers the one
thing the victim needed: *someone answered.* That "someone answered" is the wow.

**The Dial spine (why this also scores criterion 2):** the story exercises every
primitive — **inbound SMS** + **inbound voice** (she reaches us), the **AI voice
agent** (calm support), the **outbound escalation call** (human + emergency
contact), and the **outbound fan-out** (the same line reaching a whole city). The
emotional hero is inbound; the scale beat keeps the Dial depth visible.

## 60-second fallback (if the submission must be ≤60s)

Same backbone, compressed — protect the back half (Dial depth scores higher than
production polish):

- **Compress the cold open to ~13s:** 0:00–0:06 the unanswered helpline (drop the
  establishing stairwell shot), 0:06–0:10 "no one on the line," 0:10–0:13 the
  black *"What if someone always answered?"* turn.
- **Keep intact:** she texts/calls Aftercall and is answered with real Hebrew
  audio (~8s), the support→distress read (~6s), the red escalation call (~12s),
  the scale pull-back (~10s), the close (~8s).
- **Cut entirely:** the quiet "phone in hand" cost beat (0:14–0:20). The
  unanswered line already carries the cost.

## Must-have vs. cut-if-short

- **Must-have (3 shots):** the unanswered-helpline problem, **she contacts
  Aftercall and is answered** (real Hebrew audio), the red escalation call. These
  carry impact + Dial depth + human-in-the-loop.
- **The narrative cold open costs ~20s at 90s / ~13s at 60s.** It's the emotional
  engine — but if you're over time, the cold open compresses before the back half
  loses a single Dial beat.

## Alternate cold-open lines (same visuals)

- **Stakes/human:** "In the worst hour of her life, she called for help — and got
  hold music. *Times ten thousand.*"
- **Provocation (phone-native):** "A blackout takes out every app on this screen.
  It doesn't take out a phone call." — sharpest single sentence we have; good as a
  mid-video punch.

## Recording checklist

- **Reads on mute.** Every Dial primitive (inbound SMS, inbound voice, the AI
  support agent, the outbound escalation call, the outbound fan-out, webhook-driven
  dots) must be legible from captions alone.
- **Sell the isolation, not gore.** The cold open works because she's *alone on
  hold*, not because anything is graphic. Quiet room, hold music, a face falling.
- **The hero shot is "someone answered."** Linger on the moment her text gets a
  reply / the calm voice comes on — that exhale is the emotional payoff.
- **Real audio once.** One genuine Hebrew Dial exchange on the "she's answered"
  beat; pre-record a clean backup clip in case the network dies during capture.
- **Hard cuts + captions, no narration track.** Public Loom/YouTube/Vimeo link.
- **Freeze-hour discipline:** storyboard locked before recording; one good take of
  each must-have shot beats ten loose ones.
